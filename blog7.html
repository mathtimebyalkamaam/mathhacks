How to Study for a Math Exam (The Ultimate Guide)

Posted on October 10, 2025 by Alka Ma'am

The math exam is coming. For many students, those words can cause a lot of stress. The most common reaction is to ignore it until the night before, and then try to cram everything into your brain in one marathon study session. This is the #1 mistake students make!

Studying for a math exam is like training for a big sports match. You can't just show up on game day without any practice and expect to win. You need a game plan. This guide will give you a simple, powerful 3-phase plan that works for any student, from Class 5 to Class 10. Let's learn how to study smarter, not just harder.

First, The Mindset: Be a Calm Captain

Before you even open a book, you need to have the right mindset. Panicking is the worst thing you can do for a math test. When you panic, your brain's working memory—the part you need for solving problems—shuts down. A calm mind is a smart mind.

Remind yourself: math is a skill, not a talent. Nobody is born "bad at math." You get good at it by practicing, just like you get good at cricket or drawing. Believe that you can improve, and you are already halfway there.

Phase 1: The Explorer (2 Weeks Before the Exam)

In this phase, your only job is to understand the concepts. You are not trying to solve hundreds of problems yet. You are an explorer, making a map of all the topics you need to know.

  1. Review Your Map: Read through your class notes and textbooks. Don't try to memorize everything. Just read to understand the main ideas in each chapter.
  2. Find the Bumps: As you review, make a special list of the topics that feel confusing. Use a red pen. This is your "Weak Spot" list. Be specific! Don't just write "Fractions." Write "Adding fractions with different denominators."
  3. Fill in the Gaps: Now, focus only on that list. Watch videos (like on our YouTube channel!), ask your teacher for help, or work with a friend. Your goal is to have no confusing spots left on your map.

Goal of this phase: Don't leave any topic as a complete mystery. Understand the 'why' behind the rules and formulas.

Phase 2: The Builder (1 Week Before the Exam)

Now that you have your map, it's time to start building your skills. This phase is all about practice, but there's a right way to do it.

  1. Use the Staircase Method: Don't jump straight to the hardest problems. Start with the easiest exercises to build confidence. Once you can do them easily, move to the medium problems. Only then should you try the hardest ones.
  2. Don't Look at the Answer Key: Struggle with each problem for a few minutes. This is what builds the connections in your brain. Only look at the answer if you are completely stuck.
  3. Keep a "Mistake Notebook": This is your secret weapon. When you get a problem wrong, write it down and explain in one sentence *why* you made the mistake. ("I forgot to distribute the negative sign," "I used the wrong formula for area.")
  4. Spaced Repetition: Don't do 50 problems from one chapter in one day. It's better to do 10 problems from 5 different chapters. This forces your brain to remember things for longer.

Goal of this phase: To turn your understanding into a real skill. Practice is how you get faster and more accurate.

Phase 3: The Athlete (2 Days Before the Exam)

This is the final preparation for game day. You're not learning new things now; you're sharpening your skills and getting your mind ready for the pressure of the exam.

  1. Take a Full Practice Test: Find a sample paper. Set a timer for the same amount of time as the real exam. Sit in a quiet room with no phone. This simulates the real exam and helps you practice working under pressure.
  2. Review Your Mistake Notebook: This is the most important step! Read through your mistake notebook one last time. This reminds you of the specific traps you tend to fall into.
  3. Create a "Formula Sheet": Write down all the important formulas and definitions on a single page. You can't take it into the exam, but the act of writing it all down is one of the best ways to memorize it.
  4. Get a Good Night's Sleep: Don't stay up all night cramming! A tired brain can't solve problems. The best thing you can do the night before is rest.

Goal of this phase: To build confidence, practice time management, and do a final review of your weak spots.

Game Day: During the Exam

You've trained hard. Now it's time to perform. Here's how to stay calm and do your best.

  • The First 5 Minutes: Don't start writing immediately. Read the entire exam paper. Put a star next to the questions that look easy, a check next to the medium ones, and a question mark next to the hard ones.
  • Start with the Stars: Do all the easy, starred questions first. This builds your confidence and gets you some quick marks in the bank.
  • Don't Get Stuck: If a question is taking more than a few minutes, circle it and move on. You can come back to it later. It's better to solve three other problems in that time.
  • Show Your Work: Neatly write down every step. Even if you make a mistake and get the final answer wrong, you can still get partial marks for showing the correct method.
  • The Final Check: If you have time at the end, go back and check your work. Start with the questions you were unsure about. Look for silly mistakes!

Tips for Your Class

While the 3-phase plan works for everyone, here’s how to adapt it for your level:

  • For Classes 5-6 (The Adventurer): Focus most of your time on Phase 1. Make sure you really understand the 'why'. For fractions, fold paper into halves and quarters. For geometry, find shapes around your house. Turn studying into a game.
  • For Classes 7-8 (The Detective): Phase 2 is your most important phase. You need to practice many different types of problems to see the patterns. Try teaching a concept like linear equations to a parent. If you can explain it simply, you really know it.
  • For Classes 9-10 (The Architect): You need to balance all three phases. Your problems are more complex. Spend time in Phase 2 on the most challenging, multi-step problems. Your "Mistake Notebook" is critical for avoiding small errors in trigonometry or polynomials that can cost you big marks.

You Are Ready!

Success in a math exam doesn't come from one night of frantic studying. It comes from a smart, steady plan. By starting early and breaking your preparation into these phases, you can walk into that exam hall feeling confident, calm, and ready to show what you know. You can do this!
